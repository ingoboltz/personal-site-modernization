Sporadic Musings - Chapter 2

Edited slightly years later to remove identifying details and keep the focus on personal experience rather than the parties involved.

"The life span of a consulting promise"

or

"On the shortest consulting career ever"

Dear all,

As I had only just recently churned out my long-planned and overdue Swiss report, you might have expected the usual period of silence. In my new, time-consuming and supposedly demanding job as a management consultant for a large established consultancy, I was bound to have little time over the next few months — years? — to indulge my literary ambition. The Australia report would clearly have to wait.

Alas, how quickly things can change. Here I am, with ample time on my hands, typing away. So before I proceed to share my travel experiences with anyone willing to read, I will briefly detour into what must surely rank as the shortest consulting career I have yet heard of.

Strutting into the office on my first day of work, I was running on that mixture of anticipation and worry that grips you when you start something new. I was ready to dive into the consulting world open-eyed and without all the prejudices I had accumulated about "the suits" over time. During my studies, the prospect of working for the big-name firms of this world had seemed like the ultimate pinnacle of professional success. Later, in the real working world, I found that many people spoke of them rather differently: as expensive slide-makers, elegant bearers of borrowed certainty, and highly polished insurance policies for nervous executives.

So which of these, or what in-between, would they — would I — turn out to be?

Things started out nicely. A bunch of flowers welcomed me, a large office was to be shared with just one other colleague, my name was already on the door, and an assistant was there to help me with administration of all sorts. There was a good cafeteria, free drinks, fruit and cereals, and even a free sushi lunch announced. I was impressed.

The office itself was pretty empty, but with consultants often spending their week at client sites, I was not surprised. The people who were around were friendly. I had been told to meet a director, who would brief me on my first assignment, and to meet my practice head the following day.

As well as it all had started, things slowed down considerably after that. The director never got around to meeting me, being too busy to spare half an hour. My practice head flew into town the next day and actually found a whole 30 minutes between various calls to talk to me. Interestingly, he did not seem to have much of a concrete plan for how my mobile-Internet experience would be used, which surprised me, since that had been central to why I had been hired. I was referred back to the director to help with a pitch, though nobody seemed sure whether it would involve my area at all. Other than that, there was to be another meeting two days later, unless I was happy to wait an unspecified number of hours while more calls took place. As it was 9pm by that time, I decided that meeting in two days would do nicely.

In the true spirit of continuity, nothing happened for the rest of the week. The director was busy, my practice head postponed the meeting, and I was sitting on my arse in my empty office. I kept myself busy by emailing half of my new company regarding mobile-commerce work and people active in the field. I found a fair number of them — though scattered all over the bloody globe — and a few interesting projects, though many had already wrapped up and not much new seemed to be starting. I also heard that a more implementation-focused group had been wound down — the sort of group I had quietly hoped to migrate toward in time.

That worried me a bit, since part of what had attracted me was the possibility of doing real work rather than merely discussing it in polished decks. Still, I remained optimistic that if I could link up with the competent people I had identified, there would be plenty of scope for learning and doing something worthwhile.

So when my practice head rushed in to "talk about something unpleasant" on Friday evening, I still had no clue. When he started talking about business pressure and tough decisions, I felt that little twinge in the stomach. When he announced that cuts had been decided and that he would have to let me go, I was dumbfounded. He assured me it was nothing personal, said he was sorry, mentioned that others had been affected as well, handed me a paper, offered whatever help he could provide — and that was that. My consulting career had lasted barely longer than a carton of milk.

What left me especially puzzled was how quickly it had all happened. Some of the paperwork seemed to have been prepared almost before I had even found the coffee machine. The decision may well have been driven by events larger than me, but from where I stood the whole thing had a dreamlike, pre-arranged quality to it: I had entered a play whose ending had already been written.

Then came the strangest part.

A few days into my second week, I was still nominally attached to the office because I was to assist with some work during the notice period. One morning I found my computer access blocked. I was shown a printout of an online post I had made shortly after being told I was out — an emotional description of what had happened from my side. It was said that the post had damaged trust and caused concern internally. There was talk of serious consequences and of immediate steps that might be taken.

It took me a full day to work through the initial shock enough to stop feeling nauseous and start feeling properly angry. Then I did what people do when suddenly cornered by corporate language and implied menace: I began reading, asking questions, taking informal advice, and trying to understand where I stood. The more I looked into it, the less intimidated I felt. So I waited.

For a while, nothing happened.

I opened my mailbox every day with a small shiver, expecting a letter. After some time, one arrived — and contained only my payslip. No mention of the affair. I relaxed. I thought perhaps cooler heads had prevailed and everyone had decided to let the thing subside.

I was wrong again.

Some weeks later, I received a call and was asked to come into the office. Why? There was apparently "an agreement" for me to sign. Nobody seemed especially willing or able to explain what it was. "Just come in and sign."

So I went in, and an administrator presented me with a polite smile and a document. The first page was harmless enough: paid leave for the remainder of the notice period, some boilerplate, nothing remarkable. Then came the second page. I was being asked to sign a broad undertaking not to discuss the episode publicly, together with a sizeable contractual penalty in case of breach.

I was flabbergasted.

This went well beyond ordinary confidentiality as I understood it. It did not feel like a request to protect legitimate internal business matters; it felt like an attempt to put a lid on an embarrassing story. I was not willing to sign it, and I said so.

That answer was not greeted with enthusiasm. It was suggested to me that signing would be the easy way to close the matter, that everyone would benefit, that things could become unpleasant if I refused. I responded that, from my point of view, the paid notice period was not some benevolent favor but simply the existing arrangement, and that if anything further was being asked of me, I would need a very good reason to agree to it. The atmosphere cooled considerably.

At one point, I explained as calmly as I could how the whole sequence had felt from my side: first being recruited with great promise, then being dropped almost immediately, and now being presented with a silence document as though the main problem in all of this were not what had happened, but that I might speak of it. I also said that I had neither the time nor the inclination to wage some public campaign, but that if I was expected to sign away my right to describe my own experience, that was another matter entirely.

That was more or less the end of it.

There were references to further responses and sterner reactions from higher up. There were dark hints about "next steps". There were solemn predictions that people would not be happy. Then: silence. Eventually another letter arrived with the final administrative odds and ends, and the whole strange little opera came to its end.

So, what is the outcome of all this?

Despite having been thoroughly mugged by events career-wise, I at least had several weeks of paid time and walked away battle-hardened and less easily impressed by corporate theater. But one does have to admire the sequence in all its absurdity. This was not some chaotic backyard operation. This was one of those polished institutions whose entire business rests on the appearance of judgement, stability and professionalism. And yet the experience, from where I stood, unfolded roughly like this:

STEP ONE: I was recruited away from a reasonably safe and respected role, with talk of interesting projects, international possibilities, development, training, and serious work.

STEP TWO: Before resigning my previous position, I had sought reassurance about the state of the market and the outlook. The impression I came away with was that things were solid enough and that I was not stepping into an immediate trap. So I resigned.

STEP THREE: My old company, upon receiving notice of my departure, made a counteroffer — financially attractive and more senior in character. But by then I had already committed, and in accordance with my perhaps misguided sense of integrity, I stuck to my word and declined it.

STEP FOUR: I started work, wandered about for a week in an office where nobody quite knew what to do with me, and then found myself on the way out before I had even had the chance to prove useful.

STEP FIVE: After I shared my frustration online in a moment of understandable outrage, internal concern suddenly focused less on what had happened and more on the fact that I had described it.

STEP SIX: Some time later, after the dust had settled a little, there was a final attempt to tidy the whole matter away with a document that would have kept me publicly silent. I did not sign it. And that, finally, was that.

It was, at the very least, an efficient way to educate me about how quickly professional mythology can collide with reality.

At one point, a journalist contacted me about the online post. For a brief moment I was tempted to tell the whole story. Then I declined. Nobody would really be helped by turning the matter into a miniature public scandal. The people who might suffer would not be the grandees, but the junior staff and decent colleagues who had treated me perfectly well during my brief stay. And besides, I had said I did not intend to wage a media war over the thing, and I preferred to keep my own word, whatever I thought of everyone else's.

So here I am, wondering whether my guardian angel is trying to tell me something about career choices. I had received mixed reactions from you out there regarding my transformation into a suit-and-tie consulting high-flyer. Some approved and congratulated, others questioned my sanity and suspected I had mutated into someone completely different while we had not spoken. I myself had worried that I might become harder, more cynical, and more drawn into surfaces and gamesmanship than I liked. I remember sitting in a café late after my second day, watching severe chins and stony expressions pass by in navy suits, and wondering whether that was my future. I silently promised myself I would get out the moment I felt my chin beginning to metamorphose.

And yet, ultimately, I had wanted to find out for myself what life inside that strategy-consulting "elite" felt like, rather than rely on second-hand judgement. Well: now I had first-hand material, if only in concentrated form. I was angry, tired and disappointed. I was not sure where to go from there.

Maybe it would be a small tech company, where I could have some real impact, produce more than colorful slides, and leave the suit in the wardrobe. Or maybe I would do something quite different, such as exploring that lingering ambition to work for an interesting NGO. Who knows. But I was bruised. A little harder. A little more cynical. A little less trusting. And a little less the person I wanted to be.

This was one experience I could very happily have done without.

Best regards,

Ingo
