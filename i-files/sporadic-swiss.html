Sporadic Musings - Chapter 1

Edited slightly years later to remove identifying details and keep the focus on personal experience rather than the parties involved.

The @ventures — Notes from the restless and revolutionary Internet economy

The last time I chased my fingers across a keyboard to share my life with you out there, I was recovering from my African re-entry culture shock. Things were confusing, and big things lay ahead.

I was struggling with my old "new" environment and with the challenge of finally getting completely off mom's wallet and onto my own financial legs. I was to enter the workforce, become a member of the proletariat, and end my days of happy life without department bosses, office intrigue and tax returns. About a year later, here I am, feeling like a battle-hardened veteran of the working world, and once more standing at what might turn out to be a significant fork in the road. But more on that later.

I had tested the waters in the semiconductor industry while in Belgium, and the monotony of the processing research work had not exactly raised my enthusiasm about working for a chipmaker as an engineer. Getting into the business side of that industry was difficult if you did not come out of the engineering pool. So I was looking for alternatives.

Starting out in the New Economy

Perhaps predictably, considering the hype around this time last year, I ended up in the New Economy. Not exactly by design, though, but rather by accident. A new-found friend whom I met on the beaches of Mozambique while still drifting through Africa enthusiastically recommended a Berlin Internet agency he had just signed with. Cool atmosphere, young people, shaping the revolution that was to turn the world upside down — I was sold. Especially after a brief but unsettling encounter with recruiters from a very large traditional consultancy, I was sure that I was on the right path.

So I moved to Berlin, found a little flat in a trendy student area of town, and became a dot-commer — complete with two dogs at the company, screaming blue and red neon lights in the comfortable lobby, and staff with an average age of about 22.

During my interview, I had not been able to nail down what exactly I was to be doing in my job as a project manager; since pretty much everybody at my new employer was a "PM", there was a lot of flexibility. But things started out with great speed. On my second day, I joined three colleagues on a pitch for new business. Excitement pure, client contact from the start.

Unfortunately, the vibes were pretty bad during the meeting. We did not really agree on much, and when we returned that day, it was clear to me that we would not get this client and that I would be working on something else. I was wrong.

The next four weeks (!!) I spent as an office refugee without a project or any work at all, being told that I needed to "wait in the pipeline" until something started. I upped my email frequency, surfed the web extensively at T-1 speed, and subscribed to a million tech newsletters to keep myself busy. After four weeks, I was starting to go nuts with boredom and was getting close to the point where I was ready to leave the company.

In a last-ditch effort, I went out to look for ANY work myself. Talking my way around the company, I found that one of the biggest projects going at the time needed someone who knew at least a little about mobile Internet technologies. I had some clue about the field from hobby reading, so I screamed "here!" and was invited to the project.

I figured that I had finally understood how things worked in the new economy: namely that you needed to find your work yourself instead of being "assigned" the old-economy way. I expected that at the meeting with our unit head, which I had requested more than a week earlier and which obviously had not had enough priority to happen sooner, I would simply get confirmation and a go-ahead. I was wrong again.

What followed was a drubbing for "going behind his back" and not waiting to be assigned by him, plus an initial refusal to let me join the project. Not exactly that he had an idea where I should go, either. After a fair amount of lobbying and back-and-forth, I managed to claw my way into it anyway, but by the time I was in a plane on my way to Zürich, my image of the glorious new economy had already developed a few gaping cracks.

The Swiss Experience

So I was on my way to the airport the next Monday morning. Funny enough, even though I had hoped to find some stability and continuity after all the drifting in Africa, I ended up footloose again — shuttling between Zürich and Berlin in a weekly rhythm. As a result of this, I never really arrived in Berlin. I also never really settled down in Zürich either. I spent nine months torn between two locations, also a new experience for me, since I had been used to being uprooted and planted anew, yet not to sitting between two such chairs.

I was also to dive into the biggest, most complex and difficult project I had ever seen from the inside. My agency, a large consulting partner, the client, and a number of subcontracting companies were all trying to work together to create an ambitious new digital platform — something that was meant to do many things at once, and do them all in a way that was supposedly far ahead of its time.

In the end, it did not come together. After many months of work and a rapidly changing market, much of the original ambition never reached the real world. The programme was eventually stopped, the grand vision was shelved, and many of us went home with the uneasy feeling that a great deal of effort had gone into work that would never quite find its place in the world.

So was it a good experience? Indeed it was. What I took away from that very expensive education was that, if you want a project of that size to have a chance, a few basics matter enormously:

• Know in some detail, before you start, what problem you are actually trying to solve and what "done" is supposed to look like.
• Keep egos and inter-company rivalries under control, or they will quietly become the job.
• Putting more people on a project does not automatically mean things will happen faster.
• Have one clearly accountable person steering the ship — someone empowered to make decisions, hold people to them, and keep everyone facing the same direction.

From where I sat, too few of those basics felt firmly in place, and the programme drifted accordingly.

Nevertheless, I had been lucky enough to work with a small team of coders, pretty much left alone by everyone else, to create a pretty cool mobile application. My "baby" never saw the light of the online world and disappeared with the rest of the project when it was shut down. But the process of creating it, improving and revising it, defending it within the project against all those people who figured that "we didn't need mobile anyway", and working with everyone to make it happen, was a great experience. I made a few good friends in the process, picked up some valuable skills, and raised that most important, fulfilling and meaningful factor of my personality — my market value.

In addition, I got to swim in the beautiful and clean Zürich lake, ski the Swiss Alps, hike mountain passes, savour excellent cheese and brilliant chocolate, and eat loads of Swiss fondue and Geschnetzeltes. I watched rich people in fine coats parade across Bahnhofstrasse. I rubbed shoulders with private bankers who count money in "boxes" — wherein one "box" equals one million Swiss Francs — and who send you to a proletarian retail bank if you arrive at their doorstep with anything less than two of those boxes.

I used trams which were ALWAYS on time, went through train tunnels so long that you could finish War and Peace while passing through one, and which criss-cross the Swiss Alps like holes in Emmentaler cheese, making you wonder why they have not collapsed yet. I got used to attaching a hearty "li" to any noun available in German in order to blend in with the local dialect, finding that one of the few words never "cute-fied" by adding the "li" was the mighty "Franc", which ties in nicely with the fur coats and the "boxes".

I got to experience Swiss feeling for ORDER, the extent of which reduces any German countryside town to an anarchic hippie commune. I saw a cleaning lady clean my apartment UNDER the fridge, ON TOP of the doors, and for a whole 14 hours, only to declare it "clean by Swiss standard". I was reprimanded in writing for "opening several windows of my apartment, notably on the western, northwestern, southern and southeastern side repeatedly during the course of the day, resulting in considerable loss of energy".

I was charged a whopping 25 francs cover for Kaufleuten, only to find that this did not include ascending to the balcony level, which was reserved for VIPs. I watched Basel people reject Halloween commercialism in favour of the good old traditions of Fasnacht carnival, which includes getting up at 4am to parade huge handmade lanterns adorned with hand-painted political satire cartoons that have been created over the course of a whole year in people's clubs. I slept in a hotel room that had a hammock in it. I spent six weeks wrapped in the most resilient Zürich fog I have ever experienced. Oh yes, and I ate Birchermüesli, which you MUST try whenever it crosses your sight on a menu anywhere.

There is way too much I saw to put down here, but "those days in Zürich", accompanied by a nostalgic sigh by all those who have been there, just about captures the Swiss experience.

Unfortunately, with all the beauty of Zürich, while on the project I also often felt oddly unsupported by my company. In the chaos of the project, when I had needed guidance, I often got little; when I would have liked backup from Berlin, it seemed far away. There was not much structured feedback on the work I was doing, and after a while I felt a need for change. I wanted some structure, some training, someone with a good many years of experience to learn from.

So I started listening more closely to the job offers floating around, the propositions being made, and when an offer finally appeared that seemed very good indeed, I struck a deal. I was to become a consultant.

At the announcement of this to my closer friends, I earned much ridicule, some horrified faces, and a fair amount of head-shaking. After all, moving from a chaotic new-economy company — complete with two dogs, offices full of life-size figures of Darth Vader and Lara Croft, and a work structure and hierarchy that often seemed nonexistent — straight into one of the grand old houses of management consulting, with the suits, the ties, the polished shoes and chiselled chins, seemed quite lunatic and incompatible indeed.

On the other hand, international assignments were looming, much better pay, and that structured learning effect, along with proper feedback on how well I was doing, loomed large and proved too tempting in the end. I knew I had to try, or I would be asking myself forever after: "What if I had...?" Ultimately, I had little choice but to try.

As a nice side-effect, I got to take all my vacation, plus some overtime I had accumulated, and disappear into the AUSTRALIAN sunset for a whopping TWO months! The trip, from which I have only returned a week ago, has been truly excellent, full of beauty, insights and great experiences, and I hope to publish another report on it should I find the time. In any case, I have a new favorite city on this planet — SYDNEY!! — and a country too beautiful and fascinating to stay away from for too long. Wait for my move down under...

So here I am, having a slow start into the much-labelled and emotionally charged field of management consulting. I am a little scared, and I hope that if I find my chin becoming chiselled, or myself turning into more and more of a "suit" in that unfavorable meaning, I will have the wisdom to see it and the willpower to pull out. In the meantime, I will take the money, do the job, and if one thing is sure at the end of this, it is that I will finally have learned how to tie a tie.

Always yours, and happy about any responses,

Your Ingo
